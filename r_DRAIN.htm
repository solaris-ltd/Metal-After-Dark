Kick-ass crossover - 98%

DRAIN - Living Proof

Interestingly, as a hardcore punk fan, I’ve never been hugely into crossover. But DRAIN’s 2023 full-length, ‘Living Proof’ has slowly been changing my mind. The cover art would have me thinking this was a punk album if I hadn’t been informed of DRAIN’s crossover style. From start to finish, this album keeps the speed and the energy at full throttle (except for a certain intermission track which I’ll have something to say about) and as we approach the halfway mark of the year, I have no hesitation putting Living Proof in the running for my album of the year.

Right off the bat, the thrash and groove influence is evident. Opening with “Run your luck”, the track contained a surprisingly clean breakdown in the last twenty or so seconds, which came as a pleasant surprise to me, and I could immediately tell this wasn’t just a run-of-the-mill thrash album, nor was it straight up Pantera-worship (which the very Dimebag-esque solo had me expecting). The instrumentation exhibits their unique musicianship, however. The bass tone is charmingly gritty and the drummer demonstrates their skilled use of the kit at any and all tempos. Quoting frontman Sammy Ciaramitaro, “Straight up, no one is having more fun than me when we’re up there!” and you can just hear in every line he spits, screams and shouts this could not be more true. Blending a perfect mix of punk and thrash, any fan of either genre can properly enjoy the best of both worlds throughout the brief twenty-five-minute duration of the album.

I wouldn’t be able to move on without a mention of the ‘Intermission’ track. It’s… out of place. On a thrashy, punky, crusty album, no one would expect a rap track. But Intermission (ft. Shakewell) is just that. I physically breathed a sigh of relief when the rap section paused and the band came in and played what may be the catchiest riff across the entire album. Granted, the riff follows the melody of the piano that introduces the track and consequently, the rap sequence. And ok, maybe the isolated “yo DRAIN!” before the drop into an ear-melting breakdown is pretty fuckin’ badass. But that first forty seconds of rapping that really doesn’t belong here? Not the most enjoyable forty seconds of listening. Maybe the catchy riffs and breakdown soften the blow, but it doesn’t really cancel it out and these forty odd seconds are probably the one thing holding me back from scoring Living Proof a perfect review.

One thing I love about Living Proof that should definitely be recognised is its skilful use of riffing over speed. Instead of mindlessly pumping out blast beat after blast beat, DRAIN focuses on the groove influence a little more than the thrash. As I mentioned, the riffs are nothing short of catchy. They’re punchy and simultaneously smooth and cleverly lead into some impressive half-time breakdowns. The breakdowns are done to perfection as well. Without overdoing it an obnoxious amount and completely ruining a track, DRAIN instead pounds on ears mercilessly.

‘Good Good Things’ was another interesting track breaking away from the established sound of the album so far. It almost felt like a throwback to a nostalgic 2000s sound but unlike Intermission, it works! I’ll never skip Good Good Things on a listen. It further goes to show the sky’s the limit when it comes to DRAIN’s musicianship.

A somewhat disappointingly small runtime of twenty-five minutes is all we get from DRAIN this time, but I think it’s perfectly fair considering the ideas they execute, the riffs they shred and the energy they perform with. The punk thrashers one-up themselves from California Cursed and have succeeded in changing my mind on crossover. I look forward to whatever they put out next and will continue exploring more crossover in the meantime!
